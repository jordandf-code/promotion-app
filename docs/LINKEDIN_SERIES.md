# LinkedIn Campaign Plan

## The thesis

The tools were toys. Now they're not. If you're a senior leader who dismissed AI after trying ChatGPT once, the threshold crossed while you weren't looking — and the skills that matter on the other side are the ones you already have.

## Who this is for

Senior leaders in professional services (Partners, MDs, VPs at IBM, Big 4, Accenture, and similar firms) who are already in Jordan's network. This is not an algorithmic reach play. This is an eminence play with a specific market of ~200-500 people who already follow Jordan. The goal is to be the person in their network who made them think differently about AI — not as a technologist, but as a peer.

## Who this is NOT for

Developers. AI enthusiasts. Career-changers learning to code. Startup founders. Bootcamp graduates. If these people engage, fine — but they are not the target audience and the content should never be optimized for them.

## What Jordan built and why it matters (context, not content)

Jordan built a production web app (Career Command Center) using AI-assisted development (Claude Code) in 3 days — multi-user auth, AI integration, 93 tests, deployed to production. He is not a developer. He is an Associate Partner at IBM Canada.

The app is the *evidence*, not the *point*. The point is: a management consultant applied structured thinking, systems discipline, and delegation skills to AI — and got production-quality output. The technological threshold crossed. The skills that matter now are the ones senior leaders already have.

## The vulnerability angle

Jordan always runs his career like a campaign. Every promotion cycle: metrics, senior sponsors, a brand to build, a story to tell. The tools for pulling that together don't exist. He didn't want to spend a month on a PowerPoint package — he wanted to think about it smarter. This isn't about being unprepared. It's about tools not existing for what senior professionals actually need.

This framing is permanent and defensible. It doesn't expire if a specific promotion doesn't land on a specific date. "I always run my career like a campaign" is a statement about how Jordan operates, not a bet that can fail publicly.

**Do NOT declare a specific promotion target or timeline in any post.**

## Core principles

### Voice and authorship
- **All words are Jordan's.** Claude helps with structure, sequencing, feedback, and editing — never writes the posts. AI-generated LinkedIn posts read like AI-generated LinkedIn posts. Everyone can tell.
- **Write as a senior leader talking to senior leaders.** Not a consultant posting about coding. Not an AI enthusiast. A peer saying: "I did this, here's what I learned, here's why you should care."
- **Be a practitioner, not a coach.** Partners write from experience with systemic insight. They don't motivate, inspire, or teach from above. They share what they saw and what it means.

### What to show, what to protect
- **Show real artifacts.** Screenshots, code snippets (styled via Carbon/Ray.so), Claude Code conversations. Make it tangible.
- **Be honest about failures.** AI was confidently wrong. Code shipped with bugs. Trust-but-verify isn't just for audits. The honesty is the credibility.
- **Do NOT share the "74 hours in 3 days" number.** It signals distraction from client work, not hustle. Abstract to "a few intense weekends" or don't quantify time at all.
- **Do NOT frame the app as a side project.** Frame it as a proof of concept for how senior leaders will work in the future. The app is evidence of a thesis, not a hobby.
- **Do NOT overclaim novelty.** Ethan Mollick has been making the "manage AI like a co-worker" argument for two years. Jordan's differentiation is the vantage point (senior consulting, real stakes, real build), not the concept. Be in conversation with existing thinking, not claiming to have invented it.

### Intellectual honesty guardrails
- **Do NOT claim "technical knowledge doesn't matter."** The honest version: "The skills that mattered most were ones I already had — but I can't fully audit what I can't fully understand, and that's a real tension." Acknowledge what you can't see.
- **Do NOT frame 416 messages as "data."** It's a story, not a study. "I tracked 416 interactions" is a hook. "The data from 416 messages shows..." is a claim that invites methodological criticism. Use the number as narrative, not evidence.
- **Do NOT position AI as a replacement for engineering.** Position it as a multiplier for domain expertise — with caveats. "AI didn't get good enough to replace your skills. It got good enough to be multiplied by them — with real limits I'm still learning."

## Format

### Not a labeled series
No "Part 3 of 8." No explicit series branding. A body of posts over 4-6 weeks that share a coherent worldview without requiring the audience to track sequencing. Each post stands alone. The narrative arc is implicit — readers who see multiple posts recognize a throughline. Readers who see one post get full value.

### Blog + LinkedIn hybrid
- **Write long-form pieces on a personal blog** (or LinkedIn Articles as fallback). These are the published artifacts — referenceable, forwardable, includable in a promotion case.
- **Post a LinkedIn text post linking to each piece** with a sharp hook and a key insight. The LinkedIn post is the trailer; the blog is the film.
- **The algorithm penalty for external links doesn't matter** because the target audience is already connected. Distribution to second-degree strangers is not the goal.

### Volume
Write as many posts as there are genuinely distinct insights a senior leader would find valuable. Not a predetermined number. If that's 5, write 5. If it's 8, write 8. Quality and distinctness over cadence.

### Timing
- Post Tuesday or Wednesday, 8-10 AM ET
- Block 60-90 minutes after posting to reply to every comment substantively
- Space posts at least 4-5 days apart — no pressure to maintain a rigid schedule

## Post concepts (working list, not fixed)

Each concept below is a candidate. Jordan should write the ones he has something genuine to say about and cut the rest.

### "The threshold crossed."
The anchor post. AI tools were toys — Jordan tried them, they didn't deliver, he moved on. Now they do. The skills that matter on the other side aren't technical: they're structured thinking, clear communication, domain expertise, and knowing when to push back. This is the "memos vs. email in 2000" post. Wake up call to peers.

### "Managing AI is managing."
How Jordan managed Claude the same way he manages a consulting team: broad delegation, decisive course-correction, trust-but-verify. The explicit parallel to junior consultant management. Acknowledge the limits of the analogy (AI can't push back, no reciprocity) in one sentence to show sophistication. Be in conversation with Mollick's framework, not claiming to have invented it.

### "I brought consulting methodology to building software."
Plan-first discipline. Dependency graphs. Structured phases. 200 planning checkpoints. The skill that mattered most wasn't coding — it was knowing how to break a problem apart before touching anything. Carousel candidate: framework showing methodology applied to AI output.

### "What I actually built — and why the numbers matter."
The scope post. 44 commits, 387 files, 93 tests, multi-user auth, AI integration, deployed to production. Comes AFTER the "how I think" posts so the audience already understands why the scope is credible. Screenshots of real functionality. Frame metrics as evidence of production discipline, not as impressive to engineers (they're not — 44 commits is a Tuesday for a dev. They're impressive because a non-developer produced them with real quality controls).

### "The tools for running your career don't exist."
Why Jordan built it. Every promotion cycle: metrics to track, sponsors to align, a brand to build, a story to tell. The tools for that are spreadsheets and slide decks. Not "I had no system" — rather "the system doesn't exist, so I built one." This is the professional services resonance post. Every senior leader relates to this gap.

### "Claude was confidently wrong. Twice."
The failures post. 24 buggy first implementations. 16 wrong approaches. A specific anecdote (Claude insisted docs were synced — they weren't). The lesson: AI requires the same quality control you'd apply to any deliverable. Trust but verify. Show a real failure and how it was caught. This builds credibility and earns the right to be taken seriously on the positive posts.

### "What 416 interactions taught me."
The framework post. What behaviors predicted good output: clear problem framing, plan-first discipline, knowing when to push back, domain expertise. What didn't predict it: technical background. Frame as "what I noticed" not "what the data proves." Carousel candidate. Acknowledge the limits: n=1, personal experience, not a study.

### "What would you build if the tools actually worked now?"
The closer. Bring it back to the reader. The point isn't disrupting SaaS or replacing developers. It's: if you want something for yourself, you can build it now. The skills are the ones you already have. Offer to share the project setup (CLAUDE.md, methodology doc) for anyone who wants to try. CTA: "What's the tool your firm should have built five years ago?"

## Where Claude helps (and where it doesn't)

**Claude helps with:**
- Structuring the narrative arc across posts
- Reviewing drafts for flow, clarity, and consistency with this plan
- Identifying where a draft overclaims, undersells, or drifts into the wrong audience
- Pressure-testing whether a post would land differently with the target audience vs the wrong audience
- Suggesting what to cut (Jordan will over-explain)
- Checking against the guardrails in this document

**Claude does NOT:**
- Write the posts
- Generate the voice or tone
- Produce the final published text
- Make creative decisions about which posts to write or what angle to take

## Rubrics for Claude to evaluate drafts against

When Jordan shares a draft post, evaluate against ALL of the following:

### Audience check
- [ ] Would a Partner/MD at a professional services firm find this valuable?
- [ ] Would this post make sense to someone who has never used Claude Code?
- [ ] Is this written peer-to-peer (senior leader to senior leader), not teacher-to-student?
- [ ] If this post goes viral with developers and AI enthusiasts, does it still serve the primary audience?

### Vulnerability check
- [ ] Does this post contain a specific promotion target or timeline? (REJECT if yes)
- [ ] Does it frame Jordan as unprepared/disorganized, or as someone who built a better system because existing tools are inadequate? (must be the latter)
- [ ] Could a competitor, client, or promotion committee member read this and form a negative impression? Specifically which sentence and why?
- [ ] How does this post age if the next promotion cycle doesn't go as planned?
- [ ] Does it reveal time investment in a way that signals distraction from client work?

### Intellectual honesty check
- [ ] Does any claim go further than the evidence supports?
- [ ] Is "416 messages" or any number framed as data/evidence rather than narrative/experience? (flag if yes)
- [ ] Does the post claim or imply that technical knowledge is unnecessary? (reframe if yes)
- [ ] Does the post claim originality for an idea that Mollick, McKinsey, or others have already published? (acknowledge prior art if yes)
- [ ] Is there an honest acknowledgment of limits or tensions, not just wins?

### Lane check
- [ ] Does this read like a consultant posting about coding, or a senior leader talking about a shift in how work gets done? (must be the latter)
- [ ] Is the app positioned as evidence of a thesis, or as a side project/hobby? (must be the former)
- [ ] Would the reader's takeaway be "I should try this" or "Jordan is good at coding"? (must be the former)

### Craft check
- [ ] Is the hook in the first 2 lines strong enough to stop a senior leader mid-scroll?
- [ ] Is the post under 300 words (for LinkedIn text) or under 1500 words (for blog)?
- [ ] Does every paragraph earn its place — could any be cut without losing the point?
- [ ] Is there exactly one CTA, and is it a specific question rather than "thoughts?"
- [ ] Are there 0-2 emojis maximum?
- [ ] Are hashtags (if any) at the bottom, max 3-5?
- [ ] Does it sound like Jordan, not like AI?

### Format check
- [ ] If this is a LinkedIn text post linking to a blog, does the LinkedIn post stand alone with a complete insight?
- [ ] If this includes code or technical content, is it framed around the business decision, not the implementation?
- [ ] If this is a carousel, is it 6-9 slides with one concept per slide?
- [ ] Are any external links placed in comments rather than the post body? (for LinkedIn-native posts)

## How to use this document with Claude (web or API)

When using Claude on the web (claude.ai) to workshop posts:

### Setup
Paste this entire document into the conversation as context at the start of your session. Say: "This is my campaign plan. I'm going to share drafts of LinkedIn posts. Evaluate each one against the rubrics at the bottom of this document. Be direct — tell me what's wrong before telling me what works."

### Drafting workflow
1. **Dictate or type a rough draft.** Don't polish — get the idea down. Say "here's a rough draft" so Claude knows not to treat it as final.
2. **Ask for rubric evaluation.** Claude will check against all rubrics and flag issues.
3. **Iterate.** Tell Claude which feedback you agree with and which you don't. Claude adjusts.
4. **Ask "who is the person most likely to criticize this post, and what would they say?"** This pressure-tests from the attacker's perspective.
5. **Ask "read this as a Managing Partner at [firm]. What's your reaction?"** This checks the audience filter.
6. **When you're satisfied, ask Claude to suggest cuts.** Jordan over-explains. The best version of every post is shorter than the first draft.

### Key phrases for directing Claude
- "Attack this draft" — Claude evaluates harshly, finds weaknesses
- "Who would hate this?" — Claude identifies the hostile reader and their objection
- "Read this as [persona]" — Claude evaluates from a specific audience perspective
- "Is this my voice or AI voice?" — Claude flags sentences that sound generated
- "What would Mollick say?" — Claude checks whether a claim overlaps with existing published thinking
- "Cut 30%" — Claude identifies what to remove while preserving the point
- "Am I overclaiming?" — Claude checks intellectual honesty guardrails specifically
